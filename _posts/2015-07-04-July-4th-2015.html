Buffer: The North Star

I recently watched this interesting talk from Facebook's VP of Growth, Alex Schultz. He emphasized that the key to Facebook's growth is that they have a very distinct and clear goal as a company: to connect the world.

He refers to this as Facebook's "North Star". Every decision they make as a company is made with this goal as their top priority. He credits Facebook's success to this type of focus. It made me realize that I am in need of my own "North Star".

During a job interview last week for a junior developer position, my interviewer gave me some terrific actionable and specific feedback. He told me that he was not going to hire me and the primary reason was for a lack of passion. During our face to face interview which lasted approximately an hour, I did not convince him that I was passionate about technology and understanding how things worked. He said that he was looking for a developer who not only viewed technology as a career but also as a hobby.

In the moment it hurts like hell to receive that kind of honest and direct feedback. I had to start asking myself some honest questions. What was I passionate about? Why did I switch careers? What does my dream company look like?

My dream company is Buffer. I discovered this amazing company from a blog post in Mattermark's daily newsletter.

The thing I love most about Buffer is their transparency. Everything in the company from salaries, to revenue, to equity, to how they set their pricing, to fundraising details, and more is completely transparent. They even make all of their code open source! These are things that most companies guard as a top secret treasure, afraid of sharing these details with anyone, even people within the organization.

This type of transparency makes me want to work for Buffer because I know there will not be any bullshit games negotiating my value as an employee or office politics. These are things I have dealt with in other companies, and hate. By being open about the inner workings of the organization, it makes it easy to feel like it's your company. I am not even an employee of Buffer and I feel vested in the company's success similar to how I feel vested in the success of the Philadelphia Eagles. I can't help but root for a company that treats their employees right and is on a mission to make the world a better place.

Transparency is only one of the company's 10 core values. Each value makes me want to work for Buffer more.

Buffer is currently hiring developers and I have a strategy to familiarize myself with their stack and work on understanding their open source code. My hope is that with hard work I will be working for Buffer within 6 months. But even if that does not happen, I now realize that what I want most from my next job is a company with a strong value system that matches my own. Buffer helped me find my North Star.

Jekyll Blog

The most important code I wrote this week was configuring Jekyll for my blog. Jekyll is a static site generator. It has simplified the process of maintaining a blog.

Before Jekyll my work flow to write a new post consisted of me copying and pasting a blank HTML template to a new file, writing the post, and then copying and pasting the link to my new post to the side bar and manually updating the index page of my blog to display the most recent post.

This process was annoying and time consuming. With Jekyll, my new work process consists of me writing a new post in either markdown or HTML and pushing it to GitHub. Jekyll has also allowed me to modularize the layout of my blog into individual files making all of the static pages on my site easier to maintain.
